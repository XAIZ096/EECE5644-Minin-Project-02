Requirement 2: Predicting Medical Charges (Regression)
Setup
Using the encoded dataset (one-hot encoded sex, smoker, region), I split the data 80/20 into training and test sets (random_state=42), yielding 1069 training rows and 268 test rows. Features were standardized using StandardScaler for the model that requires it (SVR).

Key Findings

Simple Linear Regression, using smoker_yes as the single strongest predictor identified in EDA, achieved R²=0.673 alone — confirming smoking status is the dominant cost driver.
Multiple Linear Regression improved R² to 0.807 by incorporating age, BMI, children, and region.
Polynomial Regression was tested at degrees 2–4 to capture nonlinear effects. Degree 2 performed best (R²=0.883); by degree 4, train R² kept climbing (0.867) while test R² fell (0.800) — clear evidence of overfitting.
Ridge and Lasso, tuned across α = [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 50, 100, 200], both performed best at the lowest α tested (0.01), nearly matching unregularized Multiple Linear Regression. This indicates the base model wasn't overfitting significantly, so regularization offered little benefit. Lasso's coefficients at α=0.01 did not zero out any features (smoker_yes dominated at ~23,078; all other coefficients were nonzero but much smaller).
SVR required scaling the target variable (charges) in addition to features — without this, both kernels performed worse than a mean baseline (negative R²) due to the default epsilon being too small relative to charge magnitudes. After fixing this, RBF kernel reached R²=0.879 (best MAE overall at 2547), while the linear kernel reached R²=0.798.
Decision Tree Regression was tuned across max_depth = [2, 3, 4, 5, None]. Performance peaked at depth=4 (R²=0.897) and declined at unrestricted depth (R²=0.819), again demonstrating overfitting with excessive model complexity. The tree's top split was on smoker_yes, followed by age and bmi — consistent with EDA findings.

Recommendation
Decision Tree Regression (max_depth=4) is recommended to Ms. Nabil: it achieved the highest R² (0.897) and near-lowest MAE (2621) among all seven model types, showed no signs of overfitting versus deeper trees, and — unlike SVR or polynomial regression — is directly interpretable. The tree can be shown to the board as a simple decision path (e.g., "smoker → age → BMI"), making it easier to justify pricing decisions to non-technical stakeholders than a black-box or coefficient-heavy alternative.

Requirement 3: Flagging Expensive Customers (Classification)
Setup
"Expensive" was defined per the finance team's rule: charges above the dataset median of $9,386.16. This produced a nearly balanced binary target (669 vs. 668 customers). Split 80/20 with stratification to preserve class balance in both sets.

Decision Tree depth was tuned across [2, 3, 4, 5, None]; accuracy peaked at depth=5 (0.940) and dropped at unrestricted depth (0.910), consistent with the overfitting pattern seen in the regression task.
Key Findings

Logistic Regression achieved balanced error (14 false positives, 11 false negatives out of 268 test cases).
Decision Tree (depth=5) achieved substantially higher precision (0.992) — when it flags a customer as expensive, it is correct 99% of the time — at a modest cost to recall (0.888 vs. Logistic Regression's 0.918), meaning it misses slightly more truly expensive customers than it falsely flags cheap ones.

Recommendation
Decision Tree Classifier (max_depth=5) is recommended: higher overall accuracy, very high precision (minimizing false alarms that would misprice low-risk customers), and the same interpretability advantage as the regression model. The one caveat worth flagging to the stakeholder: if the cost of missing an expensive customer outweighs the cost of a false alarm, Logistic Regression's higher recall may be preferable — this is a business tradeoff, not a modeling one.
